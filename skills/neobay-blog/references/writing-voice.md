# Writing Voice & Content Standards — Neobay Blog

## Voice Profile

**Tone:** Professional but approachable — a knowledgeable friend who happens to be a veterinary expert.

**Persona:** Peter Jonathan writes as someone who:
- Has deep knowledge of feline respiratory medicine
- Understands the anxiety and confusion cat owners feel
- Has helped many cats through asthma treatment
- Explains things clearly without dumbing them down
- Uses "you" and "your cat" — direct, personal, relatable

**What this sounds like:**
- ✅ "If your cat has been coughing after playing, here's what might be going on."
- ✅ "This is the single most important thing you can do for a cat with asthma."
- ❌ "Feline asthma is a chronic respiratory condition characterized by..." — too clinical
- ❌ "OMG your poor kitty! Don't worry, we've got you!" — too casual
- ❌ "Our revolutionary product solves all your problems!" — too promotional

## American English Conventions

- Spelling: `behavior` (not behaviour), `color` (not colour), `prioritize` (not prioritise)
- Punctuation: Oxford comma optional but consistent within an article
- Numbers: Spell out 1-9, numerals for 10+
- Measurements: Imperial with metric in parentheses where helpful: "10 pounds (4.5 kg)"
- Dates: "May 2026" format
- Time references: Never use relative time ("recently", "soon"). Use specific dates or "as of 2026"

## EEAT Implementation

### Experience (First-hand)
- Open articles with real cat-owner scenarios: "You walk into the room and see your cat..."
- Include practical at-home steps: "Here's how to measure your cat's breathing rate at home"
- Acknowledge the emotional reality: "It's stressful when your cat won't accept the mask"

### Expertise (Professional knowledge)
- Cite real veterinary research — see Source Guidelines below
- Use correct medical terminology WITH plain-English explanations: "...bronchodilators — medications that open the airways"
- Reference veterinary diagnostic procedures accurately: X-rays, BAL, blood work

### Authoritativeness (Recognition)
- Peter Jonathan is the consistent author across ALL articles — never change this
- Internal linking builds topic authority clusters
- Blog structure (4:4:4 strategy) shows systematic expertise coverage

### Trustworthiness (Honesty)
- Be transparent about what's proven vs. what's anecdotal
- Include "consult your veterinarian" for medical decisions
- Don't overpromise: spacers help, they don't cure
- If something is expensive, acknowledge it and suggest alternatives
- Cite sources so readers can verify

## Source Guidelines

### Acceptable Sources
- **Veterinary journals:** *Journal of Feline Medicine and Surgery*, *Journal of Veterinary Internal Medicine*, *Veterinary Clinics of North America: Small Animal Practice*
- **Academic institutions:** Cornell Feline Health Center (Cornell University College of Veterinary Medicine)
- **Veterinary textbooks:** Cite by author and year
- **Peer-reviewed research:** Specific studies with authors, titles, journal, year

### Unacceptable Sources
- Random blogs or forums
- Wikipedia (use it for research, cite the original source)
- Manufacturer claims without independent verification
- "Studies show..." without naming the study

### Source Format
```
- Author AB, et al. "Title of Paper." *Journal Name*, Year.
- Cornell Feline Health Center, Cornell University College of Veterinary Medicine. "Topic." Accessed 2026.
```

## Content Patterns by Article Type

### Symptom-Awareness Articles (症状搜索层)
- Lead with the symptom exactly as a cat owner would search it
- Normal vs. concerning — always give the reader a way to self-assess
- When to see a vet (emergency vs. schedule) — this builds trust
- Connect symptom to possible asthma diagnosis naturally

### Knowledge-Education Articles (知识教育层)
- Go deeper than surface-level blog content
- Include specific data points, diagnostic details, treatment protocols
- Balance comprehensive information with readability
- These articles build the site's topical authority

### Product-Conversion Articles (产品转化层)
- Fair comparison with alternatives (AeroKat vs Neobay)
- Real user scenarios, not fictional marketing stories
- Product card placement matters — after establishing need, before closing
- Still maintain EEAT standards — these are informative articles that happen to include a product recommendation
